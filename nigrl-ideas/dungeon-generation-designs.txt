==========================================================================================
SECTION 1 — METH LAB REFINEMENT: INDUSTRIAL LINEAR LAYOUT
==========================================================================================

CONCEPT
-------
The Meth Lab reads as a converted industrial building — a warehouse or commercial kitchen
turned clandestine drug operation. The key visual: a wide central corridor (the "main line")
with rectangular lab modules branching off both sides, like a spine with ribs. Some modules
share walls and bleed into each other through wide openings. A few oversized rooms mark
important waypoints: the entry loading dock, a central cook room, and an end-of-floor
chemical storage vault. Secondary rooms nest behind the primary labs, accessible only by
passing through them — rewarding exploration but also funnelling players into ambushes.

ALGORITHM — LINEAR CHAIN WITH BRANCHING SIDE ROOMS
---------------------------------------------------
1. Carve the main corridor: a 3-wide horizontal hall running roughly left-to-right across
   the map (y-centered), occupying about 80% of the horizontal span. The player enters at
   the left end; the down-stairs appear at the right end behind the final room.
2. Along the corridor, pick 6-10 evenly-spaced "rib attachment points" alternating above
   and below the corridor.
3. At each attachment point, carve a RectRoom of variable size (min 5x4, max 10x7) adjacent
   to the corridor wall. The room is connected by punching a 2-3 tile wide gap into the
   corridor wall (not a 1-tile door — a proper opening).
4. For 30-40% of primary side rooms, carve an additional nested room adjacent to the far
   wall of the primary room. The nested room has only a single gap connecting to its parent.
5. Place 1-2 "major rooms" (12-16 wide, 8-10 tall) at the corridor's midpoint and far end.
   These are the cook room and storage vault. They connect to the corridor via wide openings
   (3-4 tiles) and may have interior pillar or table obstacles.
6. No MST step — connectivity is guaranteed by the corridor itself. Optional: add 1-2
   extra connections between adjacent side rooms that happen to share a wall, creating
   loop paths without the main corridor.
7. Spawns: guards cluster near the cook room and storage vault. Tweakers and chemists
   scatter through the labs. The loading dock (entry room) has crates and loot.

IMPLEMENTATION NOTES
--------------------
- Replace generate_crack_den() with generate_meth_lab() in zone_generators.py.
- The main corridor is a HallRoom (horizontal, length ~54, width 3) centered vertically.
- Side rooms are RectRoom or PillarRoom instances. Store them in dungeon.rooms as normal.
- "Wide opening" = call _carve_rect on the shared wall segment (2-3 tiles wide) instead
  of carve_corridor. The opening is not a door; it's just absent wall tiles.
- Mark the entry room (rooms[0]) and the vault/cook room for special spawning logic in
  spawn_meth_lab().
- Nested rooms: store a parent_room_index attribute on the Room if needed by spawn logic
  to know that the nested room is harder to access and should have better loot.

-------------

appears on and vary the stagger to prevent memorization across runs.


==========================================================================================
SECTION 2 — CASINO: OPEN FLOOR PLAN (BSP)
==========================================================================================

CONCEPT
-------
The Casino is one continuous open space divided into functional zones by interior partitions
and furniture layouts, not walls with corridors between them. You can see across the whole
floor from most positions, which changes combat dramatically — enemies spot you from far
away, but you can shoot across the room and plan routes visually. The floor is large and
mostly open with clusters of tables, slots machines (represented as '#' obstacles), a bar
at one end, and a VIP lounge behind a partial partition. Doors in the Casino are wide
archways (3 tiles) rather than 1-tile openings.

ALGORITHM — BSP + WIDE OPENINGS (Room-in-Room variant)
--------------------------------------------------------
Option A: Classic BSP
1. Recursively split the dungeon bounding box by alternating horizontal/vertical cuts.
   Stop splitting when a partition is smaller than a minimum threshold (e.g. 12x10).
2. Each leaf partition becomes a "zone" (slot floor, card tables, bar, VIP).
3. Carve ALL leaf partitions as floor — there are no walls between leaves except the
   shared partition lines.
4. On each shared partition wall, punch 2-4 wide openings (3 tiles each), leaving
   short wall stubs between the openings. This gives the feel of open casino floor
   while still creating meaningful sightline breaks.
5. Interior obstacles: place clusters of 1-tile '#' pillars representing slot machines,
   table clusters (2x1 '#' pairs), and a 1-tile bar counter in the designated bar zone.

Option B: Room-in-Room (simpler to implement with existing code)
1. Carve the entire interior as floor (one large RectRoom sized 60x45).
2. Place interior partition "walls" — short 1-tile-thick wall stubs (3-8 tiles long)
   that divide the space into sections without fully enclosing any zone.
3. Add clustered obstacle groups: 2-3 tile wide clumps of wall tiles for slot machines,
   table arrangements, etc.
4. Place the bar as a U-shaped wall enclosure open on one side.
5. VIP room: a small RectRoom at the back with a 3-tile opening and 1 guard.

This guide presents Option B since it requires no new generator class.

IMPLEMENTATION NOTES
--------------------
- generate_casino() in zone_generators.py: carve the master floor rect first, then
  place interior obstacles via _carve_rect calls that SET tiles back to TILE_WALL
  (reverse carving) to create partitions and obstacles.
- "Obstacle clusters" for slot machines: a 2x3 block of TILE_WALL tiles.
- The bar counter: a 10x1 strip of TILE_WALL, leaving a 2-tile gap at each end.
- Wide archways at partition intersections: naturally formed by leaving gaps in the
  partition stubs.
- dungeon.rooms should still contain logical room references for spawn/loot placement.
  Use notional sub-rooms (Rect bounding boxes over each zone) even though the floor
  is continuous — these are spawn zones only, not carved rooms.
- Enemy AI: wander_ambush works well here since the open floor gives long LOS.
  Guards near the VIP room should use room_guard mode with a patrol rect.

-------------

extremely dangerous because the player cannot break LOS. Conversely, Blackkk Magic
and gun builds shine here because of the long unobstructed lines. Melee enemies
clump up and are easier to kite. Consider restricting the Casino to floors where
the player is likely to have some ranged capability (floor 5+).

SECTION 3 — UNDERPRISON: CELLULAR AUTOMATA CAVERNS
==========================================================================================

CONCEPT
-------
The Underprison is below street level — part abandoned correctional facility, part natural
cave system that broke through the old basement. The layout reads as organic rock chambers
connected by narrow, winding passages, but with rectangular cells (the old prison cells)
embedded in the cave walls. Cells still have bars (single-tile wall segments with a gap).
Lighting is sparse. Dead ends are common — the player must backtrack. Some chambers are
flooded (water tiles). The down-stairs might be at the bottom of a pit, reachable only
after finding the right passage through the caves.

ALGORITHM — CELLULAR AUTOMATA + EMBEDDED PREFAB CELLS
-------------------------------------------------------
Phase 1: Seed
  Fill the dungeon with random noise: each tile is TILE_FLOOR with probability P (P=0.45).
  Leave a 2-tile wall border at all edges.

Phase 2: Smooth (5-7 iterations)
  For each tile, count living neighbors in a 3x3 Moore neighborhood.
  Apply rule: if count >= 5 → TILE_WALL; else → TILE_FLOOR.
  (Classic "5-rule" automaton. Produces cave-like blobs with narrow connectors.)

Phase 3: Flood-fill connectivity check
  Find the largest connected floor region via flood fill.
  Punch 1-tile-wide corridors between any disconnected regions and the main region.
  This ensures the entire map is reachable. Keep these connectors as-is (do not smooth
  them — they become the narrow winding passages).

Phase 4: Embed prison cells
  Select 4-8 random wall clusters adjacent to open cave area.
  Carve 3x4 RectRooms (cell size) at these locations.
  Place a 1-tile "bar" wall in the opening to each cell — a wall tile with a 1-tile gap
  beside it (the cell door). If the cell is already accessible from the cave, place the
  bar wall 1 tile inside the room.

Phase 5: Water placement
  For 1-2 low-lying chambers, flood fill a small area with TILE_WATER (new tile type,
  or use existing TILE_FLOOR with a water marker). Impassable or slows movement.
  Alternatively use cosmetic '~' floor tiles if TILE_WATER is not yet implemented.

Phase 6: Register rooms
  Flood-fill the largest cave regions and register them as CavernRoom instances
  in dungeon.rooms so that spawn/loot logic can use them.

IMPLEMENTATION NOTES
--------------------
- This is the most complex generator. Add generate_underprison() to zone_generators.py.
- The CA loop runs on dungeon.tiles directly: seed → iterate → flush.
- "Embedded cells" are carved AFTER the CA pass, so they appear as distinct rectangular
  pockets in the organic cave wall. They will naturally contrast with the cave shape.
- Cell bars: place TILE_WALL at (cx, cy) and leave (cx+1, cy) as TILE_FLOOR. Two bars
  spaced 2 apart creates a classic prison cell front.
- Flood-fill connectivity: simple BFS from the first open tile. Any floor tile not reached
  is either isolated (wall it off) or connected via a 1-tile punch.
- dungeon.rooms[0] = entry cave region, rooms[-1] = deepest chamber with stairs.
- Recommended: 2-3 CA iterations for less-connected caves (more dead ends), 5-6 for more
  open caves. Use floor_num to tune: deeper floors = fewer iterations = tighter caves.

BALANCE NOTE
------------
CA caves tend toward high-branching-factor layouts — many rooms but uneven connectivity.
Dead ends should reward exploration: place guaranteed loot in dead-end pockets. The prison
cells, being cramped, are natural one-on-one combat arenas. The flooded chambers create
navigational puzzles if water slows movement. CA is sensitive to its seed probability P:
lower P (0.40) = more walls, fewer connections; higher P (0.50) = large open blobs.


==========================================================================================
SECTION 4 — BOTANICAL WEED GARDEN: ORGANIC RING LAYOUT
==========================================================================================

CONCEPT
-------
The Botanical Weed Garden is a hidden greenhouse/grow-op, either rooftop or underground.
The layout has a central hub room (the main grow tent or central irrigation hub) with
radiating paths leading to individual garden chambers. Each chamber is a separate growing
area — some are rectangular hydroponic bays, some are more organic outdoor-style plots.
The paths between chambers are not straight corridors but gently curving walkways (achieved
with diagonal or slightly irregular carving). Scattered obstacles in the garden zones
represent planters, grow bags, and trellises. Some open areas have dense '%' vegetation
tiles that slow movement or block vision. Water channels '~' run through some chambers.

ALGORITHM — HUB AND SPOKE WITH ORGANIC SMOOTHING
-------------------------------------------------
Phase 1: Hub room
  Place a CircleRoom (radius 5-6) at the approximate center of the map.
  This is the control room / main grow station. It is always rooms[0].

Phase 2: Spoke placement
  Choose 5-8 evenly-spaced angles around the hub (e.g. 0, 45, 90, 135, 180, 225, 270, 315
  degrees). For each spoke angle, compute a target point at distance 12-18 tiles from hub
  center along that angle.

Phase 3: Garden chambers
  At each spoke endpoint, place a room. Alternate between:
  - RectRoom (5x6 to 8x8) — hydroponic bay
  - CircleRoom (radius 3-4) — organic circular garden plot
  - DiamondRoom (radius 3) — unusual shaped plot (a nice garden shape)
  Reject placements that overlap existing rooms (apply standard padding check).

Phase 4: Spoke corridors
  Connect each garden chamber back to the hub using the existing carve_corridor() function.
  Additionally, add a random "wander" to the corridor: instead of a straight L-corridor,
  pick a midpoint offset by +-2 tiles from the L-bend, creating a slightly dog-legged path.
  This gives the walkways an organic feel without full drunk-walk complexity.

Phase 5: Cross-connections
  Connect 2-3 adjacent chamber pairs with short corridors (bypassing the hub). This
  creates loops and prevents the hub from being the only bottleneck, giving the player
  more tactical options for circling enemies.

Phase 6: Vegetation obstacles
  Inside each garden chamber, place 30-50% of floor tiles as '%' vegetation tiles.
  These are TILE_FLOOR (passable) but have a special marker that: (a) blocks LOS beyond
  1 tile (like low brush), or (b) applies a slow effect. Use a separate set or dict
  dungeon.vegetation_tiles: set[tuple[int,int]] to track these.
  Place them in irregular clusters (a small drunk-walk blob seeded at a random room tile).

Phase 7: Water channels
  In 1-2 chamber rooms, carve a 1-tile-wide row/column of TILE_WATER ('~') tiles as
  an irrigation channel. These are impassable obstacles, forcing the player to navigate
  around them.

IMPLEMENTATION NOTES
--------------------
- generate_botanical_garden() in zone_generators.py.
- Hub room is a CircleRoom; spoke chambers are mixed types as above.
- The "vegetation tile" concept is new to the engine. Simplest implementation: a
  dungeon.vegetation_tiles: set[tuple] that render.py draws as '%' instead of '.'.
  Engine checks this set when computing LOS if the "blocks LOS" property is desired.
  If not implementing LOS blocking immediately, vegetation can be purely cosmetic.
- Spoke corridors: carve_corridor() already does L-shaped carving. For the organic
  variant, call _carve_wide_corridor(from_point, midpoint_offset) then
  _carve_wide_corridor(midpoint_offset, to_point) where midpoint_offset is the L-bend
  location randomized by +-2. Width=1 for narrow paths, width=2 for main spokes.
- Cross-connections between adjacent chambers: compute which pairs are within distance
  threshold (15 tiles center-to-center) and connect 2-3 of them.
- dungeon.rooms layout: [hub, garden1, garden2, ..., gardenN].
  rooms[0] = hub = entry and exit point (or separate entry ante-room).

BALANCE NOTE
------------

creates a push-pull dynamic: venture out down a spoke, clear the garden, retreat if
injured. Vegetation density should be tuned per floor: early floors have sparse '%'
tiles; deep floors have thick brush that enemies can hide in. The water channels in
certain chambers create forced narrow-pass encounters. Consider placing the staircase
in the chamber directly opposite the entry direction (player enters from north, stairs
are in the south chamber) to encourage traversing the whole map.

SECTION 5 — TECHNIQUE REFERENCE GUIDE
==========================================================================================

Each technique is rated:
  Difficulty: Easy / Medium / Hard / Very Hard (relative to existing NIGRL codebase)
  Best for: which NIGRL zone type it suits

---------------------------------------------------------------------
1. RANDOM PLACEMENT + MST (CURRENT SYSTEM)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  Place rooms at random positions with overlap rejection. Connect them with a minimum
  spanning tree (Prim's algorithm) to guarantee connectivity, then add a few extra
  random connections to create loops. NIGRL uses 11 room shapes (Rect, L, T, Cross,
  Circle, U, Diamond, Cavern, Hall, Oct, Pillar).

How it works in NIGRL:
  generate_crack_den() calls each room shape's carve() method, then build_mst() returns
  index pairs, then carve_corridor() or _carve_wide_corridor() punches the connections.

Pros:
  - Highly varied output across runs; every floor feels different.
  - Easy to tune room mix (weights per zone, per floor).
  - MST guarantees no isolated rooms without extra complexity.
  - Well-tested; existing code is stable and well-understood.

Cons:
  - No thematic coherence — a lab room might appear next to a pillar vault.
  - Corridors can be long and winding, leading to exploration fatigue.
  - "Surprise" factor decreases once the player learns the room shapes.

Difficulty: Already implemented.
Best for: Crack Den (eclectic urban building interiors, many different room uses).

---------------------------------------------------------------------
2. ROOM ACCRETION (BROGUE-STYLE)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  Begin with one room. Each new room is placed adjacent to an existing room — not at a
  random location, but touching or overlapping the current map edge. Repeat until enough
  rooms are placed. Corridors are minimal or nonexistent since rooms share walls.

How it works:
  For each new room, try attaching it to a random edge tile of the existing map. Reject
  placements that overlap previously placed rooms (except the shared edge). The dungeon
  "grows" organically from a seed room, like cells dividing.

Pros:
  - Extremely organic feel; tight interconnected layouts.
  - Very few dead-end corridors — transitions happen through room openings.
  - No MST required; adjacency ensures connectivity.
  - Good pacing: player is always one room away from the next encounter.

Cons:
  - Harder to control overall map shape — can cluster heavily in one area.
  - Requires careful overlap detection (more complex than current system).
  - Can produce very small, cramped layouts if rooms are too large.
  - Harder to guarantee minimum room count on small maps.

Difficulty: Medium (new placement loop, new adjacency detection needed).
Best for: Underprison (cramped, claustrophobic feel), or a future dungeon interior zone.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
3. BSP — BINARY SPACE PARTITION
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  Recursively split the dungeon area into two halves (alternating horizontal/vertical cuts).
  Continue until each partition is small enough to be a room. Carve a room in each leaf
  partition, then connect adjacent pairs by working up the BSP tree. Produces very
  even room distribution with guaranteed connectivity.

How it works:
  BSP node: (x1, y1, x2, y2, left_child, right_child). Split point chosen randomly within
  the middle 50% of the partition dimension to prevent tiny slivers. Leaf nodes carve
  rooms slightly smaller than their partition (padding 1-2 tiles). Each split node
  connects its two children's rooms with a corridor.

Pros:
  - Very even coverage; no area of the map is left empty.
  - Guaranteed connectivity by tree structure (no MST needed).
  - Room sizes are naturally proportional to available space.
  - Easy to reserve a specific quadrant (always put boss room bottom-right, etc.).

Cons:
  - Layouts can feel grid-like and regular, less surprising than random placement.
  - All corridors are axis-aligned; no diagonal connections.
  - Room shapes are always rectangles (or simple variants) unless you modify leaf carving.
  - Less variety per run unless BSP split choices are heavily randomized.

Difficulty: Medium (BSP tree implementation, ~80-100 lines new code).
Best for: Casino (structured, architectural feel), corporate offices, hotel.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
4. CELLULAR AUTOMATA
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  Seed the map with random floor/wall tiles, then repeatedly apply a neighbor-counting rule:
  a tile becomes wall if it has >= N wall neighbors; otherwise becomes floor. Run 4-8
  iterations. The result looks like natural caves — organic blobs connected by narrow
  passages.

How it works in NIGRL context:
  Operate directly on dungeon.tiles. After CA smoothing, run a flood-fill to detect
  disconnected cave regions and punch 1-tile corridors to join them. Register each large
  connected region as a CavernRoom for spawn purposes.

Pros:
  - Most organic-looking output of any technique.
  - Dead ends and alcoves arise naturally; rewards thorough exploration.
  - Unpredictable layout; hard to memorize across runs.
  - Cave rooms blend seamlessly into passages (no visible "room + corridor" structure).

Cons:
  - Hard to control. Connectivity fixes can create ugly 1-tile tunnels.
  - Spawn placement is harder — no clean room centers; must use flood-fill regions.
  - Can produce very large open areas OR very isolated pockets depending on seed.
  - "Rooms" (for spawn logic) must be inferred post-hoc from connected regions.
  - Might need per-zone tuning of the birth/survival rules.

Difficulty: Medium-Hard (CA loop is simple, but connectivity repair and room registration
  add complexity). ~120-150 lines new code.
Best for: Underprison caves, sewers, outdoor ruins (not built environments).

---------------------------------------------------------------------
5. LINEAR CHAIN + BRANCHING (DESIGNED ABOVE FOR METH LAB)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  Place a main corridor running roughly left-to-right (or top-to-bottom). Attach side rooms
  at regular intervals along both sides of the corridor. Some side rooms have secondary
  rooms attached to them. The player must traverse the corridor from entry to exit, fighting
  through or bypassing the side rooms.

How it works:
  Hard-code the corridor position and length. Compute N attachment points along the
  corridor. At each point, carve a side room and punch a 2-3 tile opening into the
  corridor wall. Optionally, add nested rooms behind 30-40% of side rooms.

Pros:
  - Very strong thematic clarity; reads as "factory" or "warehouse" immediately.
  - Easy to guarantee a progression from entry to exit (good pacing).
  - Side rooms create risk/reward branches: player can skip them or clear for loot.
  - Easy to place boss rooms, locked rooms, or special encounters at specific positions.

Cons:
  - Low variety between floors; the linear structure is recognizable quickly.
  - If the player is weak, the main corridor is inescapable — a death trap.
  - Nested rooms can create "surprise ambush" situations where the player is surrounded.
  - Needs per-floor variation in side room order/type to stay fresh.

Difficulty: Easy (build on existing room carving + corridor code, ~60-80 new lines).
Best for: Meth Lab, sewer tunnels, any zone with an industrial or pipeline feel.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
6. HUB AND SPOKE (DESIGNED ABOVE FOR BOTANICAL GARDEN)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  Place one central room. Radiate N corridors outward at evenly-spaced angles. Place a
  terminal room at each spoke endpoint. Optionally add cross-connections between adjacent
  spoke rooms to create loops. The hub serves as a safe chokepoint; the player controls
  the pace by choosing which spoke to explore next.

How it works:
  Hub room at map center. Compute spoke endpoints using trigonometry (angle, distance).
  Carve spoke corridors using existing carve_corridor(). Place terminal rooms at endpoints
  using existing room shape classes.

Pros:
  - Clear visual identity; immediately readable as a hub-spoke map.
  - Player agency: choose which spoke to attempt in any order.
  - Hub is naturally defensible — good for tension (player retreats to hub when hurt).
  - Cross-connections create interesting loops without sacrificing the spoke structure.

Cons:
  - The hub is a guaranteed encounter zone; players must pass through it multiple times.
  - Spoke rooms are isolated — less emergent combat (enemies rarely leave their spoke).
  - Trigonometry for placement can produce awkward corridor bends on some angles.
  - Risk of spokes clustering together if the map is too small for the chosen radius.

Difficulty: Medium (trigonometry for spoke endpoints, ~80-100 new lines).
Best for: Botanical Garden, underground cult temples, sewer junction rooms, boss arenas.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
7. VORONOI REGIONS
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  Scatter N "seed" points across the dungeon. Each floor tile is assigned to the nearest
  seed point (Voronoi region). Carve each region's tiles as floor. The boundaries between
  regions become walls. Add connections along shared boundaries.

How it works:
  For each tile (x, y), compute distance to all N seed points. Assign to nearest seed.
  Mark boundary tiles (tiles where neighbors belong to different regions) as walls.
  Connect adjacent region pairs by punching a gap in their shared boundary.

Pros:
  - Produces organic, irregular room shapes that are not rectangles or simple blobs.
  - Rooms fill the entire dungeon area evenly (no wasted space).
  - Very distinctive visual style — alien or surreal feel.
  - Natural variation in room size based on seed point spacing.

Cons:
  - Very slow to compute naively on a 68x53 grid (N*W*H distance calculations).
  - Room shapes are hard to predict; can produce very thin regions.
  - Hard to place specific room types (you carve all regions first, then assign type).
  - Less thematically clear — Voronoi shapes don't look like rooms you'd build.
  - Connecting shared-boundary pairs adds another pass of logic.

Difficulty: Hard (~150-200 new lines, plus possible performance issues).
Best for: Alien environments, drug trip sequences, surreal dream zones.
Not recommended for realistic NIGRL zones (Casino, Prison, Lab) due to irregular shapes.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
8. PREFAB TEMPLATES + PROCEDURAL FILLING
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  Pre-design a set of hand-crafted room templates (stored as lists of strings or 2D
  arrays). The generator picks and places templates at valid positions, then uses one
  of the above algorithms to fill the remaining space with procedural rooms/corridors.
  Templates can encode special tile types, enemy spawn markers, or loot positions.

How it works:
  Templates are defined as constant lists of strings (e.g. a 10x10 array of '#' and '.').
  The generator picks 1-3 templates, places them at semi-random positions with overlap
  rejection, marks those regions as "claimed", then runs random placement on the remaining
  unclaimed space. Corridors connect template rooms to the procedural rooms via MST.

Pros:
  - Specific locations (boss arenas, puzzle rooms, traps) are exactly as designed.
  - Templates can encode rich detail impossible with pure procedural methods.
  - Reduces "same-y" feel: even a random floor always has one landmark room.
  - Can encode lore/narrative elements (a specific lab layout, a crashed car, etc.).

Cons:
  - Requires authoring templates (manual work, but powerful for special rooms).
  - Templates can feel out of place if the procedural rooms are very different in scale.
  - Placement can fail if the map is crowded (need fallback to pure procedural).
  - More code needed to parse, place, and connect templates.

Difficulty: Medium (template parser is simple; placement logic reuses existing code).
Best for: Special event floors (Haitian Daycare, boss floors), boss arenas, unique
  landmark rooms within any zone.
CURRENT USE: The Crack Den already uses a lighter version of this — special rooms like
  the Dive Bar and Trap Kitchen are effectively prefab spawning patterns applied to
  randomly-selected rooms. Full template prefabs would encode the room shape as well.

---------------------------------------------------------------------
9. WAVE FUNCTION COLLAPSE (WFC)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Description:
  WFC is a constraint-satisfaction algorithm. You define a tileset of small tiles (or
  3x3 patterns extracted from a hand-designed sample map) and rules about which tiles
  can be adjacent to which. The algorithm propagates these constraints across the grid,
  collapsing each tile to a single valid type. The result is a map that statistically
  resembles the sample input while being entirely new.

How it works (simplified):
  Each tile starts as a superposition of all possible states (wall, floor, door, etc.).
  Pick the tile with lowest entropy (fewest valid options). Collapse it to one option.
  Propagate: update neighbors' possibility sets based on the adjacency rules. Repeat.
  On contradiction (a tile has zero valid options), backtrack or restart.

Pros:
  - Can reproduce highly specific visual styles from a single sample image or map.
  - Emergent patterns: the algorithm finds valid configurations the designer never
    explicitly specified.
  - Unique output every run; hard to predict.
  - Can encode very complex patterns (diagonal walls, curved rooms, etc.).

Cons:
  - Significantly more complex to implement correctly than any other technique.
  - Requires carefully designed sample patterns or a good training map.
  - Backtracking adds unpredictable generation time (can be slow).
  - Connectivity is not guaranteed and must be checked/fixed post-hoc.
  - Overkill for most NIGRL zones; best reserved for a single "showcase" zone.

Difficulty: Very Hard (~300-500 lines; needs careful design of the pattern ruleset).
Best for: A zone with a very specific repeating tile vocabulary — possibly a subway
  system, a tiled office building, or a future "dream sequence" zone. Not recommended
  as a first advanced generator; implement all others first.

Note: A simplified "tile-rule" approach (not full WFC) can be implemented more easily:
  define 5-10 tile "patterns" (e.g. wall-corner, hallway, T-junction) and select/compose
  them greedily. This achieves 30% of WFC's visual quality at 10% of the code complexity.

TILE SYMBOLS (used in all mockups above):
  # = TILE_WALL     (dungeon.tiles[y][x] = TILE_WALL = 0)
  . = TILE_FLOOR    (dungeon.tiles[y][x] = TILE_FLOOR = 1)
  + = door/opening  (just TILE_FLOOR, but called out visually as a transition point)
  ~ = water         (new TILE_WATER; or TILE_FLOOR + dungeon.water_tiles set marker)
  % = vegetation    (TILE_FLOOR + dungeon.vegetation_tiles set marker)
  S = slot machine  (TILE_WALL, 1 tile, placed as interior obstacle)
  P = pillar/planter(TILE_WALL, 1 tile)
  [] = table        (TILE_WALL, 2-tile obstacle)
  = = bar counter   (TILE_WALL, 1-tile strip)

INTEGRATION WITH DUNGEON CLASS (dungeon.py):
  - All generators work on dungeon.tiles directly (same as generate_crack_den does now).
  - Register rooms in dungeon.rooms for spawn/loot to work without changes in engine.
  - If new tile types are needed (TILE_WATER, TILE_VEGETATION), add constants to config.py
    and handle in render.py (color/char mapping) and engine.py (collision/effect checks).
  - dungeon.room_tile_map, dungeon.room_tile_map inverse: update these after generation
    so that corridor tile detection still works correctly.
  - The entry room (rooms[0]) convention: ALWAYS keep this; spawn/loot code assumes
    rooms[0] is where the player starts and skips it for monster spawning.

RECOMMENDED ROOM SIZE RANGES (for new zones to fit 68x53 grid):
  Small room:       4x4 to 6x6       (cell, closet, alcove)
  Standard room:    6x5 to 10x8      (lab, garden chamber, prison block)
  Large room:       10x8 to 16x10    (cook room, casino zone, main cavern)
  Hub room:         radius 4-6 circle (10x10 to 14x14 bounding box)
  Main corridor:    length 50-56, width 3 (for Linear Chain layout)
  Max rooms on map: 10-16 (current Crack Den range); new zones may use fewer, larger rooms.
