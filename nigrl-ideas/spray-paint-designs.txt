NIGRL — SPRAY PAINT DESIGNS
New paint items for the Graffiti / tool slot. Five designs follow.
All are tool items with charges, placed on adjacent tiles via directional key input.
One paint per tile; new paint overrides old. Fire/acid/webs coexist independently.
Enemies are oblivious to painted tiles — they never pathfind around them.

Reference: existing paints
  Red  — 10 charges. Tile amp: +25% damage TO entities on tile (double-edged).
  Blue — 10 charges. Tile buff: +5 Street Smarts, +25% spell charge preservation.

Design philosophy for new paints:
  - Each should create a distinct tactical moment. Ask: "where do I stand and where do
    they stand, and why?"
  - Double-edged paints reward planning and punish recklessness. Single-sided paints
    create safer, more readable tools with smaller, focused payoffs.
  - No two paints should do the same thing. Vary the axis: defense, speed, economy,
    crowd control, damage.

================================================================================
PAINT 1: GREEN SPRAY PAINT
================================================================================

Name: Green Spray Paint
Color (item char): '!' at (40, 200, 80)
Number of charges: 10
Double-edged: No — buff applies ONLY when the player stands on the tile.
              Enemy behavior is unaffected regardless of where they stand.

--- Effect Description ---

When the PLAYER stands on a green-painted tile, they gain a passive armor bonus:
+8 flat armor. This reduces all incoming direct damage (melee, guns, spells) by 8,
identical to how existing armor items reduce damage. The bonus is active only while
the player occupies the tile; moving off drops it to 0 immediately.

DoTs, debuffs, and toxicity ticks bypass armor (same rule as all other armor).

Green paint represents tagged territory — your turf, your ground, your rules. You
hunker down in a doorway, spray the floor, and force enemies to eat their hits into
your reinforced position.

--- Tactical Use Case ---

Defensive anchor point. Spray the chokepoint you want to hold — a doorway, a
corridor mouth, a corner — then stand in it while enemies funnel toward you. Stack
with armor from chains/equipment for a near-impenetrable defensive node. Especially
useful on late floors where enemy damage spikes.

Risk: standing still is dangerous. The player sacrifices mobility and kiting. If
flanked or if a ranged enemy exists, the static position becomes a liability.
Enemies will not avoid the tile so they will walk adjacent and attack freely.

This is the "dig in and slug it out" paint.

--- Duration ---

Permanent for the floor (armor bonus applies whenever player stands on the tile,
as long as the tile has green paint; the paint itself does not expire).

--- Floor Tile Colors ---

Visible:       bg=(15, 100, 30)   — dark forest green, readable against default floor
Explored-only: bg=(8,  50,  15)   — dim muted green

--- Balance Considerations ---

+8 armor is substantial but not broken. At baseline the player has 0 armor unless
wearing chains. A chain necklace gives roughly 5-12 armor. Green paint effectively
grants a free mid-tier chain slot's worth of armor — but ONLY while standing still.

The real tradeoff is mobility, not power level. A player standing on green paint
cannot kite, cannot retreat, cannot chase low-HP enemies. Correct play is to commit
to the tile only when the tactical geometry supports it.

On floor 1, +8 armor against Tweakers doing 4-6 damage makes you nearly immune.
On floor 4 against Bloaters doing 18-22 damage, +8 is meaningful but not dominant.
Scale appropriately across the floors.

--- Synergies ---

- Chains/necklace armor items: stacks additively → very high total armor on the tile.
- Alcoholism L5 "Last Call" (staggered debuff): lock them down while you sit on green.
- Gouge (Stabbing L1): stun adjacent enemies from your safe tile.
- Constitution investment: high max HP + high armor = extreme tankiness on the tile.
- Red paint: paint red on adjacent tiles to amp damage you deal; stand on green
  yourself for the armor. Enemies walk onto red, you hit them harder. Very strong.

================================================================================
PAINT 2: YELLOW SPRAY PAINT
================================================================================

Name: Yellow Spray Paint
Color (item char): '!' at (240, 210, 30)
Number of charges: 8
Double-edged: Yes — affects ANY entity standing on the tile, player or enemy.

--- Effect Description ---

Entities standing on a yellow-painted tile have their energy gain halved each tick.
This is a speed debuff applied via the energy system rather than a status effect.
At base 100 energy/tick, a slowed entity gains only 50 energy/tick — acting at
half frequency.

The effect is tile-continuous: as long as an entity stands on the tile, the halving
applies. Moving off the tile restores full speed immediately. Moving ONTO the tile
halves speed immediately on the next energy tick.

This represents fresh wet paint — thick, sticky, slowing anyone who steps in it.

--- Tactical Use Case ---

Zone control and corridor denial. Spray yellow in a doorway or at the mouth of a
corridor that enemies must pass through. Enemies who walk through it spend extra
turns getting to you, giving you free action windows to attack, retreat, or cast.
A slowed enemy gets fewer attacks per player turn — effectively halving their DPS.

The double-edged nature means the player must spray it on tiles they will NOT stand
on. Paint the enemy's path, then step back and fight from unpainted ground. Or
combine with standing-behind-green: paint yellow on the approach, green on your
position.

Because enemies are AI-driven and unaware of the paint, they will always walk
through it without hesitation. This is reliable, repeatable zone denial.

8 charges (fewer than standard 10) because each yellow tile is a significant
investment of map control: essentially a duration-free crowd control effect.

--- Duration ---

Permanent for the floor (the paint stays wet forever narratively, but it's paint,
and the floor is the floor). Each tile is a persistent slow zone.

--- Implementation Notes ---

  Currently: gain += entity.speed (before effect modifiers)
  Add after existing effect modifiers, before the hard minimum:

    if spray_at_entity == "yellow":
        gain *= 0.5

  This applies to ALL entities (player and monsters) since the loop iterates both.
  The hard minimum of 10 energy/tick still applies after the halving, ensuring
  no entity is completely frozen.

  The hook sits outside the status effects loop, as a separate tile-based modifier.
  Conceptually parallel to the toxicity penalty:

    if spray == "yellow": gain *= 0.5  # yellow paint

spells.py: add "yellow" to _SPRAY_COLORS.

No new Effect subclass needed — this modifies the energy loop directly.

--- Floor Tile Colors ---

Visible:       bg=(130, 110, 10)  — dark mustard yellow, warm and dirty
Explored-only: bg=(65,  55,   5)  — very dark ochre

--- Balance Considerations ---

Halving enemy speed is very powerful in the energy system. A standard Tweaker at
100 energy/tick acts once per player turn normally. On yellow paint they act once
every two player turns — effectively giving the player a free attack every other
turn.

8 charges limits total yellow tile coverage. Spreading all 8 tiles creates a
large slow zone, but the player burns the item quickly. Per-tile value is high.

The player must avoid yellow tiles entirely in most situations, which is a real
spatial planning cost. If chased to a yellow tile they slow themselves.

Fast enemies (speed > 100) are nerfed harder in absolute turns-per-player-turn
terms. A 130-speed enemy acts 1.3x, slowed to 0.65x — now slower than the player.
This makes yellow paint an especially strong counter to speed-advantage enemies.

--- Synergies ---

- Red paint: paint yellow on the approach (slow them), red closer to you (amp your
  damage when they close in). Layered control.
- Gouge: enemies already adjacent and slowed can be gouged easily since they get
  fewer actions to escape.
- Gun skills: slowed enemies give more windows for careful gun turns.
- Green paint (your safe tile) + yellow paint (their path) = core defensive combo.

================================================================================
PAINT 3: PURPLE SPRAY PAINT
================================================================================

Name: Purple Spray Paint
Color (item char): '!' at (160, 60, 220)
Number of charges: 6
Double-edged: Partially — the effect is triggered on any entity, but its payoff is
              asymmetric. On enemies it is always harmful. On the player it is only
              harmful if the player attacks from the tile, which is never forced.

--- Effect Description ---

When an entity standing on a purple tile takes any direct damage (melee, gun,
spell — via the tile_amps pathway), the purple paint "bleeds" the damage: the
attacker also takes 35% of the damage dealt as reflected damage. The attacker's
reflected damage bypasses all armor and tile amps (it is a raw HP reduction, not
a combat.deal_damage call, to prevent infinite reflection loops).

"Direct damage" uses the same gate as red paint — events routed through
apply_tile_amps / deal_damage. DoTs and status effect ticks do NOT trigger purple.

If the player stands on purple paint and an enemy melees them, the enemy takes
35% of the damage back. This creates a very strong defensive posture but only
when on the purple tile.

If the player melees an enemy standing on purple paint, the player takes 35% of
their own damage back. This punishes attacking into the purple tile carelessly.

Summary:
  - Enemy on purple, player attacks: player takes 35% back. Punishes player.
  - Player on purple, enemy attacks: enemy takes 35% back. Protects player.

The most natural use is to place purple at your feet and let enemies walk into you:
you absorb their damage while they eat reflection. Or, place purple at a choke and
do NOT stand on it or attack enemies standing on it — wait for enemies to cluster
around it, then use ranged attacks (guns/spells), which do NOT trigger purple on
the attacker since the reflection is on the DEFENDER's tile, not the attacker's.

Wait — actually: spells ARE routed through deal_damage. So spells fired at an enemy
on purple DO reflect onto the player. This makes purple a "melee magnet" not a
sniper's tool. Use guns AT enemies standing on purple at your own risk if they are
high-damage. Guns at a slowed enemy on purple from far away is still viable.

6 charges (fewest of the new paints) because reflection is the strongest defensive
effect: proper use makes the player almost unkillable while on the tile.

--- Tactical Use Case ---

Counterattack anchor. Stand on purple, let enemies come to you. Every hit they
land bounces back 35%. High-damage enemies punish themselves hardest. Works best
against melee-heavy floors where enemies funnel into corridors.

Advanced usage: paint the enemy's side of a doorway. Stand in the doorway (no
paint under you). Enemy closes to attack — they're on purple. You hit them —
you take 35% back. They hit you — you take normal damage but they take 35% back.
Net result: their damage per exchange is partially self-canceling. You have to
manage this risk consciously.

--- Duration ---

Permanent for the floor.

--- Floor Tile Colors ---

Visible:       bg=(80, 15, 120)   — deep grape purple
Explored-only: bg=(40,  8,  60)   — very dark violet

--- Balance Considerations ---

35% reflection at 6 charges is the strongest offensive-synergy paint. An enemy
dealing 20 damage per hit takes 7 reflected damage per attack — roughly 2 additional
DoT ticks worth, for free, every time they hit you.

The key limiter: the player cannot also use red paint on the same tile (only one
paint per tile). Choosing purple over red means sacrificing incoming damage amplifi-
cation. And unlike red, purple is a DEFENSIVE tool — it does not speed up killing,
it slows the player's own death.

On enemies with many attacks (Tweakers using hit-and-run, rapid-attack variants),
reflection accumulates quickly and can kill the attacker over several exchanges.

Enemies with very low damage (4-6 on floor 1) reflect tiny amounts (1-2). Less
impactful early. More impactful against late-floor high-damage enemies.

--- Synergies ---

- High CON/HP: lets the player sustain more hits and accumulate more reflection time.
- Armor (chains + green paint): absorb hits, reflect regardless of your absorption.
  Purple reflects based on the ORIGINAL damage before armor (implementation choice:
  reflect pre-armor damage for stronger payoff, or post-armor for safer numbers).
  RECOMMENDATION: reflect post-armor damage (the actual HP lost) to avoid it being
  overpowered with stacked armor. Adjust in design doc accordingly.
- Jabbing L3 Windfury: player can generate multiple melee hits on enemies on purple —
  each reflected. Net is player takes reflected damage per hit they deal, so this
  is risky and requires high HP.
- Alcoholism L5 stagger: stagger enemies to reduce their attack rate, giving purple
  less chance to reflect onto the player.

================================================================================
PAINT 4: ORANGE SPRAY PAINT
================================================================================

Name: Orange Spray Paint
Color (item char): '!' at (255, 130, 20)
Number of charges: 10
Double-edged: No — applies only when the PLAYER stands on the tile. Enemies gain
              no benefit from standing on orange.

--- Effect Description ---

When the player stands on an orange-painted tile, their melee attacks and all
direct strikes apply a burn-on-hit effect to the target. Each melee or gun hit
landing from an orange tile applies the existing "ignite" status effect to the
struck enemy, as if using a fire-attack weapon. This uses the existing IgniteEffect
system from effects.py without any modification.

Specifically: any enemy the player damages (melee or gun) while standing on orange
paint gains one ignite stack (3 turns, 1 damage per remaining stack per turn).
Multiple hits accumulate stacks, increasing DoT burn rate.

The effect is NOT a tile amp — it does not modify damage numbers through
apply_tile_amps. It is an on-hit proc checked after deal_damage resolves.
It only applies when the player is the attacker and standing on orange.

This represents fire-based graffiti — the spray itself is flammable aerosol and
the tagged tile radiates heat that ignites whatever gets touched from it.

--- Tactical Use Case ---

Offensive anchor for melee and gun builds. Plant orange under your feet, then wade
into melee range and torch everything you hit. Ignite stacks compound: hit an enemy
twice from orange, they burn for 2 damage/turn for 3 turns, then 1 damage/turn as
the first stack dies. Hitting fleeing enemies while standing on orange keeps them
burning even as they retreat.

Works particularly well with rapid-attack options (Windfury, multi-shot guns with
multiple hits): each hit from orange adds a new ignite stack.

The defensive vs offensive paint contrast:
  Green = "plant and absorb." Orange = "plant and burn."
  Both reward holding position but for opposite reasons.

--- Duration ---

Permanent for the floor. Each tile is a persistent fire-buff node.

--- Implementation Notes ---

--- Floor Tile Colors ---

Visible:       bg=(160, 65, 5)    — deep burnt orange, ember-like
Explored-only: bg=(80, 32, 3)     — dark rust brown

--- Balance Considerations ---

Ignite does 1 damage per remaining stack per turn. With one hit: 3 turns -> 3+2+1 =
6 total bonus damage. With two hits: 6+5+4+3+2+1 = 21 total across 6 turns (if not
reapplied). The DoT does not scale with any stat, making it a flat damage bonus.

Against high-HP enemies (Bloaters, Chemist), the total bonus damage is small
relative to their 80-150 HP pool. Against swarms of low-HP enemies (Tweakers,
Covid-26 at 10-20 HP), ignite stacks can finish them off passively after you've
moved on.

The key cost is positional: standing still on orange means all the same tactical
risks as green paint. Moving off drops the on-hit bonus immediately.

Orange does not increase BURST damage — it increases SUSTAINED damage through
DoT accumulation. Against a boss, stack many ignites then retreat; the DoT does
work while the player repositions.

--- Synergies ---

- Windfury (Stabbing L3): each extra hit applies ignite. 2 hits per attack from
  a stabbing weapon = 2 stacks per turn on orange. Burns compound rapidly.
- UZI / multi-shot guns: each bullet that connects = one ignite stack. UZI firing
  3-4 rounds applies 3-4 stacks in one action. Potential for very fast accumulation.
- Hot Cheetos food effect: existing fire-synergy food could stack with orange ignites.
- BIC Torch ability: both the tile and the torch apply ignite independently.
- Green paint: can't use both on the same tile. Choose: absorb hits or deal burn.
  Use green on your tile, orange on an adjacent tile you step to for offense.

PAINT 5: WHITE SPRAY PAINT
================================================================================

Name: White Spray Paint
Color (item char): '!' at (220, 220, 220)
Number of charges: 7
Double-edged: No — stat buff applies only when the PLAYER stands on the tile.
              Enemies gain nothing from standing on white.

--- Effect Description ---

When the player stands on a white-painted tile, they gain a temporary per-turn
economic bonus: every turn the player acts from a white tile, they receive a small
flat skill XP bonus applied to whichever skill tree is currently "active" (i.e.,
the skill with the most recent XP or the one with lowest completion — implementation
choice: simplest is to apply to ALL skill trees equally as a tiny passive trickle).

Mechanically: +10 potential_exp per turn to every skill tree while standing on
white. Potential exp is the currency that converts to real_exp via skill points.
This does not bypass the skill points gate — it just feeds the potential_exp pool
faster.

"The walls remember." White graffiti marks are territorial claim tags. Standing on
your turf focuses your mind, sharpens your practice, accelerates your development.

In street terms: this is the "study spot" paint. You claim a corner, hold it,
and come up while enemies try to take it from you.

--- Tactical Use Case ---

Long-duration engagement reward. If the player can maintain a position for 10+
turns, white paint generates meaningful bonus potential_exp. Over a full floor,
white on a bottleneck tile and holding that position for 20-30 turns accumulates
200-300 bonus potential_exp across all trees.

This is the most "slow burn" and low-intensity of all the paints. It does not
help win fights directly. It compounds the benefit of defensive positioning — if
you're going to stand on green paint and hold a chokepoint, paint white there too
(on a separate tile behind you, since you can't stack paints on the same tile)
and harvest XP while you wait.

7 charges allows modest coverage — enough for one or two key positions.

--- Duration ---

Permanent for the floor (the XP trickle applies each player-action turn while on
the tile). The XP boost itself is an accumulated resource gain, not a temporary
effect, so there is no "expiry."

--- Implementation Notes ---

Tile type: "white" in dungeon.spray_paint dict.

engine.py — process_action() or _run_energy_loop():

  The cleanest hook: after any player action that consumes a turn (any action
  returning True from process_action), check the player's current tile:

    spray = self.dungeon.spray_paint.get((self.player.x, self.player.y))
    if spray == "white":
        BONUS = 10
        for tree_name, skill_obj in self.skills.items():
            skill_obj.potential_exp = getattr(skill_obj, 'potential_exp', 0) + BONUS

  This is called once per player turn consumed, not once per energy tick, so it
  does not multiply with fast enemy actions. The check belongs in the post-action
  resolution, after the energy loop finishes and control returns to the player.

  ALTERNATIVE: hook into xp_progression.py as a new function
  apply_white_paint_bonus(engine) called at the end of process_action's turn-
  consuming branch. This is cleaner and keeps XP logic encapsulated.

  NOTE: 10 potential_exp per turn is small. DEFAULT_EXP_CURVE starts at 200 for L1.
  At 10/turn, it takes 20 turns of standing still to earn L1 in any tree from zero.
  In practice, skills gain hundreds of XP per fight action. White paint is a minor
  supplement during idle/defensive turns, not a dominant source.

spells.py: add "white" to _SPRAY_COLORS.
render.py: white visible/explored branches (tricky: white on light floor is low
  contrast — use the specific bg colors below to differentiate).
items.py: "white_spray_paint" entry.

--- Floor Tile Colors ---

Visible:       bg=(160, 160, 160) — medium grey (visually white-tagged floor)
Explored-only: bg=(80, 80, 80)    — darker grey (desaturated, remembered)

Note: Do NOT use near-white bg (e.g. 220,220,220) since it will be indistinguishable
from items or the player glyph on some terminal palettes. The medium grey reads as
"light tile" while remaining distinct from entities.

--- Balance Considerations ---

10 potential_exp/turn is intentionally weak in isolation. It only becomes meaningful
over long static engagements (15+ turns). The player typically spends 3-5 turns per
room in normal play. White paint rewards unusually patient or defensive play styles.

The real value proposition: white paint is the "combo tile" paint. Stack it on a
position you already want to defend (e.g., near the green paint tile). You're already
holding that position — get paid for it in XP.

At Book Smarts = 0, SP conversion rate is 10% → 10 potential_exp/turn = 1 potential
real XP/turn. At BkSmt = 50, ~34% → 3.4 real XP/turn. Neither breaks the balance
since fight actions generate far more XP.

Against the XP curve: 10 potential_exp/turn for a 30-turn floor engagement = 300.
That is 1.5 L1 skill levels of potential_exp across ALL trees simultaneously.
Meaningful but not game-breaking. On a long floor with multiple rooms and heavy
fighting, this accumulates to something noticeable in a "slow drip" way.

--- Synergies ---

- Book Smarts investment: higher BkSmt converts more potential_exp to real_exp,
  making white paint more efficient.
- Green paint (same floor, different tiles): tank on green, generate XP from white
  nearby. Tactically, paint white behind your green tile: fight from green, step back
  to white between waves.
- Smoking/Rolling skill trees: if close to a level-up, white paint can push you
  over the threshold during a defensive hold — unlocking a perk mid-floor.
- Any "stand still" build (high armor, high HP, Gouge stuns): more turns spent
  static = more turns white XP accrues.

IMPLEMENTATION PRIORITY ORDER
================================================================================

Simplest first:

1. Yellow  — pure energy loop modification, no new data fields, lowest risk.
2. Green   — requires new tile_armor_bonus field in stats.py, otherwise clean.
3. Orange  — requires on-hit hook in combat.handle_attack; uses existing ignite.
4. White   — requires post-action XP trickle hook; simple math in process_action.
5. Purple  — most complex: requires attacker param in deal_damage, raw HP reduction,
             must prevent recursive reflection. Design carefully against the call
             chain before implementing.

================================================================================
GRAFFITI SKILL TREE HOOKS (future consideration)
================================================================================

From existing memory/design: the Graffiti skill tree design (nigrl-ideas/graffiti-
skill-designs.txt) includes Throw-Up (L2) and Burner (L3) perks that amplify tile
effects. If implemented, these would multiply paint effect values:

  - L2 Throw-Up: paint tiles in a wider radius (spray hits 2-3 tiles instead of 1).
  - L3 Burner: paint effects are 20% stronger (green gives +10 armor instead of +8,
    yellow gives 60% speed reduction instead of 50%, etc.)
  - L5 Bombing Run: spray a large area in one action.

These multipliers would read from the player's Graffiti skill level in the same
places where tile effects are resolved (engine, combat, energy loop). No changes to
the item definitions — the skill tree upgrades the system, not the items.
